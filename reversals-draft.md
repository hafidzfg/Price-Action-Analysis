# Trading Price Action Reversals — Full Distillation (DRAFT)

> Al Brooks, "Trading Price Action Reversals" (2012, Wiley). Brooks terminology only. Reversal-specific knowledge — for universal concepts (bar anatomy, bar counting basics, breakout framework, 20-EMA, close importance, two legs, risk management, trade management, glossary), see [[core]]. For trend-specific knowledge, see [[trends]]. For range-specific knowledge, see [[ranges]].

---

## Quick Reference

- **Reversals are events, not states.** They occur at specific moments when the market transitions from one condition to another.
- **Four requirements for a major trend reversal:** (1) existing trend, (2) countertrend move breaking trend line, (3) test of extreme, (4) second reversal going far enough for consensus.
- **Most reversal attempts fail (~80%).** Markets have inertia. Trade with-trend until reversal is confirmed.
- **Every reversal is a failed breakout** of something (trend line, channel, flag, swing extreme).
- **Consecutive climaxes increase reversal odds.** Two climaxes → watch for correction. Three climaxes → high probability of 10-bar, two-legged correction.
- **Wedges are three-push patterns.** Third push often overshoots trend channel line. First target = bottom of pattern. Second target = measured move.
- **Final flags = last flag before reversal.** Every reversal follows some kind of flag.
- **Always-in direction matters.** Don't trade countertrend until always-in flips.

## 1. Major Trend Reversal (MTR) — Ch 3

The core reversal pattern. A major trend reversal means there are two trends on the chart with a reversal in between — a bull trend has reversed into a bear trend, or vice versa.

### Four Requirements

| # | Requirement | Details |
|---|------------|---------|
| 1 | **Existing trend** | Clear trend on the chart in front of you |
| 2 | **Countertrend move** | Strong enough to break the trend line, usually beyond the moving average |
| 3 | **Test of extreme** | Higher high, double top, or lower high at bull top; lower low, double bottom, or higher low at bear bottom |
| 4 | **Consensus** | Second reversal goes far enough for traders to agree trend has reversed |

### Key Rules

- **Every MTR begins with either a trend line break or a trend channel line overshoot and reversal.** All eventually break the trend line.
- **The trend line break does not reverse the trend.** It is the first significant sign that countertrend traders are getting strong enough that you should soon begin to trade in their direction.
- **After the trend line break, there will be a test of the old trend extreme.** The test can slightly overshoot or undershoot the old extreme.
- **Take a countertrend trade only if a reversal setup develops during this test.** If it does, the countertrend move should form at least two legs.
- **True V bottoms/tops without trend channel line overshoots are so rare they're not worth considering.** Focus on common patterns.
- **The test of the trend extreme is usually in the form of some type of channel.** The strength of the channel is especially important.
- **If the test goes too far and is in too tight a channel, don't look for a reversal.** The trend has likely resumed. Start the process over.

### Test Channel Assessment

| Channel Strength | Implication |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Tight, steep, no pullbacks** | Trend likely resuming — don't fade |
| **Many overlapping bars, bear bodies, wedge shape** | Test likely to fail → lower high/higher low reversal |
| **Noticeably less slope than original trend** | Bears/bulls gaining control |

### Overshoots vs Undershoots

- **Overshoot** (e.g., higher high after bear break, lower low after bull break) = strictly the final leg of the old trend. Forms the most extreme price.
- **Undershoot** (e.g., higher low in new bull trend, lower high in new bear trend) = pullback from first leg of the new trend.
- **Tests are frequently made of two legs** (e.g., a two-legged rally after a bear breakout).

### Probability

- **Average setup:** 40% chance of profitable swing, 30% small loss, 30% small profit. Still profitable overall.
- **Best setups:** 60% chance of profitable swing.
- **Most MTRs lead to trading ranges, not opposite trends (~70%).** But still tradable with large countertrend swings.

### Head and Shoulders Context

- Move down from head usually breaks below the bull trend line for the entire bull trend.
- Right shoulder = lower high breakout pullback short setup.
- Neckline = horizontal line across lows of pattern. Break below neckline = bears holding shorts, expecting measured move down.
- If sell-off from head to neckline is weak → entire pattern is just a large bull flag.

### Where Reversals Occur

- At significant swing highs/lows.
- At trend channel line overshoots.
- At measured move targets.
- At moving average tests after trend line breaks.
- At prior breakout points (breakout tests).

## 2. Climactic Reversals — Ch 4

A spike followed by a spike in the opposite direction. The most fundamental aspect of trading: the market is always trying to break out, then trying to make every breakout fail.

### Definition

- **Climax:** Any unsustainable behavior — trend bar or series of trend bars with relatively large ranges and little overlap. Can occur at any time.
- **Climactic reversal:** A climax that is soon followed by a sharp move in the opposite direction. All are failed breakouts of something.
- **Two-bar reversal on higher time frame.** The up and down spikes can contain many bars on your chart, but there is always a higher time frame where it's a simple two-bar reversal.

### Vacuum Effect

- Strong bears wait to short until market reaches a level where they see value (e.g., above trend channel line).
- Strong bulls wait to take profits until market reaches a level where they think it won't go higher.
- **Absence of selling creates a vacuum** that sucks the market up to the level where bears will short and bulls will take profits.
- Once both sides sell aggressively → sharp reversal.

### Consecutive Climaxes

| Climaxes | Odds of Correction | Expected Correction |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Moderate | May resume after brief pause |
| 2 | Higher | Watch for reversal setup |
| 3+ | 60%+ | At least 10-bar, two-legged correction |

- **Two consecutive buy climaxes** = effectively a double top (two pushes up).
- **Three consecutive buy climaxes** = effectively a wedge top. Can look more like a bull spike than a wedge, but same implications.
- **Pause between climaxes** can be any bar that is not another big trend bar (doji, bear body, small bar).

### Trading Rules

- **Only very experienced traders should short buy climaxes.** Easy to misread and end up shorting a strong bull.
- **After a sell climax:** Bulls take profits, bears initiate shorts, both expect lower prices for at least 10 bars and two legs.
- **Strong bulls and bears love to see an exceptionally large trend bar** after a protracted trend — they expect it to be a brief, unusually great opportunity to fade.
- **Smart traders fade likely climaxes:** Short the close of the strong bull bar, above its high, at the close of the next bar or two.
- **Weak traders do the opposite:** Weak bulls buy in panic, weak bears cover shorts — both trading on emotion against computers.

### After the Climax

- Market pauses, then either: (a) trading range, (b) channel in reversal direction, (c) trend resumes.
- **Big up + big down = big confusion = trading range** (at least for a while).
- **If bulls win:** Spike and channel bull trend with the spike being the sharp move up.
- **If bears win:** Spike and channel bear trend with the spike being the sharp move down.

### V Bottoms/Top

- Occur only a few times a month on 5-minute charts.
- **Most spike tops get tested** in a bull market, and most V bottoms get tested in a bear market, no matter how emotional and strong the reversal appears.
- Exception: If there is a strong trend and a pullback ends in a strong emotional spike, the low may not get tested. It's the bottom of a bull flag, not a trend reversal.

### Parabolic Moves

- Slope is curved and increasing (not linear).
- All are simply trend channel line overshoots and reversals.
- Often have three pushes = wedge tops.
- Best reversals have large reversal bars and preferably a second entry.
- First leg of reversal will almost always break the trend line.

### Volume

- **Daily charts:** Huge volume (5-10× recent average) at bottom = possible capitulation. Look for strong bull close.
- **Intraday:** Volume not reliable enough to trade on alone. Price action on 5-minute chart tells you what you need to know.
- **Climactic tops rarely have useful volume patterns.** Active traders paying attention to volume will miss out on good trades.

## 3. Wedges and Three-Push Reversal Patterns — Ch 5

When the market makes three pushes in one direction and then creates a reversal setup, the first target is the beginning of the pattern, and the next is a measured move based on the height of the pattern.

### Key Principle

All three-push patterns are manifestations of the same underlying market behavior:
- Wedge
- Micro wedge
- Parabolic wedge
- Wedge with fourth push
- Other triangles (including expanding)
- Triple top/bottom
- Double top/bottom pullback
- Head and shoulders
- Failed breakout below/above double top/bottom

**At some point, trend traders give up trying for a strong breakout, and reversal traders gain control. A third consecutive reversal is usually enough.**

### Wedge Pullbacks vs Reversals

| Aspect | Wedge Pullback | Wedge Reversal |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Direction | With-trend | Countertrend |
| Entry | First signal OK | Wait for second signal |
| Size | Usually 10-20 bars | Usually 20+ bars |
| Shape | Often subtle, may not look like wedge | Needs clear trend channel line |
| Context | In established trend | At end of trend |

### Wedge Shape

- **Trend line slope > trend channel line slope** = urgency. Bulls buying on smaller pullbacks, bears exiting on smaller sell-offs.
- **Converging lines** = classic wedge shape.
- **Diverging lines** = expanding triangle (same behavior, same trading).
- **Parallel lines** = stairs pattern (still three pushes, still trade the same).

### Trading Rules

- **Overshoot of trend channel line increases odds of success.**
- **If wedge reversal triggers but fails** (market extends beyond wedge extreme) → often runs quickly to measured move based on wedge height.
- **Fourth push:** Sometimes what appears to be a fourth push is really just a third push (market reset the count). Stronger momentum on second push = more likely reset.
- **Wedge tops fail when:** Traders overly eager to enter countertrend, don't wait for clear trend line break, fade the first small three-push pattern.
- **Three pushes alone, especially when small, will rarely reverse a trend** in the absence of a prior trend line break or major trend channel overshoot.

### Shrinking Stairs

- Each breakout extends less than the prior one → waning momentum.
- Increases odds of profitable countertrend trade.
- Usually signals imminent two-legged pullback.
- Can have fourth or fifth step in strong trend, but countertrend trades usually work.

### Failed Wedges

- When wedge triggers entry but fails → market typically breaks out and runs for about the same number of points as wedge height.
- **Failed wedge = breakout mode.** Whether breakout is above or below, target is measured move equal to wedge height.
- Sometimes just after failing, reverses back and creates second attempt → new trend usually protracted (10+ bars, two legs).

### Micro Wedge

- Three consecutive bars or three out of four/five consecutive bars.
- Usually results in only a minor correction.
- Bars have tails; micro trend channel line can be drawn across tails.
- Clear wedge on smaller time frame (1-minute chart).
- **In strong trend, don't trade micro wedge reversals.** Only in trading ranges or near extremes.

### Opening Wedges

- Wedges are often opening reversal patterns.
- Can follow very strong moves in the first hour.
- Sometimes very steep wedge with only slight pauses after first two pushes → abrupt reversal and trend for rest of day.
- **Never look for reversal from steep, tight wedge after first hour or two** — except as opening reversal setups.

## 4. Expanding Triangles — Ch 6

A reversal or continuation pattern made of at least five swings (sometimes seven, rarely nine), each one greater than the prior one. Part of its strength comes from trapping traders on each new breakout.

### Structure

- **Expanding triangle bottom (bullish reversal):** Enough strength to rally above last higher high (trapping longs in), then collapses to third low (trapping longs out, bears in on lower low), then reverses up forcing both sides to chase.
- **Expanding triangle top (bearish reversal):** Opposite pattern.
- **Final reversal always follows a strong leg** that breaks some significant trend line → variant of major trend reversal.
- **Third push down = lower low major trend reversal buy setup.**

### Trading

- **First objective:** Breakout beyond opposite side of triangle.
- **If breakout succeeds:** Measured move approximately the height of the last leg.
- **If breakout fails:** Pattern becomes continuation (expanding triangle flag).
- **Usually five turns before reversal**, sometimes seven.
- **Each leg is a new swing high/low in a trading range** — valid scalp setups.
- **Once fifth leg complete, swing part of position.**

## 5. Final Flags — Ch 7

Every reversal follows some kind of flag. A final flag is the last flag before the trend reverses.

### Characteristics of Final Flags

| # | Characteristic |
|---|---------------|
| 1 | Occurs in trend that has gone on for dozens of bars |
| 2 | Mostly horizontal with signs of two-sided trading |
| 3 | Several trend bars in opposite direction |
| 4 | Bars with prominent tails |
| 5 | Several reversals within range |
| 6 | Bars overlap previous bar by ≥50% |
| 7 | Can be as small as one or two bars (after exhaustive climax) |
| 8 | Tight trading ranges often become final flags |

### How Final Flags Work

- Traders see market is overdone, expect correction soon.
- They enter on breakout expecting only a scalp, not a swing.
- With everyone looking to take small profits, breakout quickly reverses.
- **Reversal can lead to:** pullback, larger correction (trading range), or trend reversal.
- **Trend reversal more likely if:** strong bear leg in flag that broke well below bull trend line, or earlier selling pressure.

### One-Bar Final Flags

- After consecutive climaxes, a pause bar (doji, small trend bar) can become the final flag.
- If next bar or two is a large spike in trend direction and then reverses → pause bar was one-bar final flag.
- Usually results in two-legged pullback lasting 10+ bars.

### Final Flag as Double Top/Bottom

- Push up just before flag forms = first push.
- Upside breakout of flag = test of that high = second push.
- If more sellers than buyers on test → market reverses down.
- **Same underlying force as double top/bottom**, but looks different enough to warrant separate classification.

### Failed Final Flags

- If reversal attempt fails and trend resumes → pullback becomes breakout pullback in ongoing trend.
- Sometimes final flag reverses briefly then forms larger flag → can break out or reverse.

## 6. Double Top and Bottom Pullbacks — Ch 8

A reversal pattern (not continuation) where the market forms a double bottom, rallies, then has a higher low pullback that tests just above the double bottom low.

### Structure

- **Double bottom pullback long:** Double bottom → rally → higher low pullback → buy setup.
- **Double top pullback short:** Double top → decline → lower high pullback → sell setup.
- **Can be thought of as a three-push pattern** where the third push didn't have strong-enough sellers to create a new low.
- **Second bottom often slightly lower than first** — common and actually preferable.

### Key Rules

- **This is a reversal pattern, not a continuation pattern** (unlike double bottom bull flag).
- **Both are long entry setups**, but one begins the trend (reversal) and the other is in established trend (continuation).
- **If second bottom doesn't reach first** → risk that bottom is only two-legged correction → better to scalp.
- **When shape is good and signal bars are strong** → one of the most reliable buy setups.
- **Move up from second bottom is usually a breakout** → pullback is breakout pullback (most reliable setup).
- **Two failed attempts to exceed prior extreme** → usually leads to reversal.

### Variations

- Triple bottom, triangle, head and shoulders bottom, final flag, double bottom pullback — **significance is the same.**
- Use loose definitions. When something resembles a reliable pattern, it will likely trade like it.

## 7. Failures — Ch 9

A failure is any trade that does not reach its goal. Every failed setup is a sign of strength by the opposite side. When a setup fails (~40% of the time), it often creates an excellent setup in the opposite direction.

### Common Failures That Lead to Opposite Trades

- Failed high 1, high 2, low 1, or low 2
- Failed breakout of anything (swing high/low, trend line, trend channel line)
- Failure to break through a magnet (measured move, prior swing, trend line)
- Failed final flag (reversal attempt fails → breakout pullback)
- Failed wedge
- Failed signal bar or entry bar
- Failed one-tick breakout
- Failed reversal (failed double bottom/top, failed head and shoulders)
- Failure to reach profit target (5, 9, 17 tick failures in Eminis)

### Failures That Are Signs of Strong Trend

- Failure to fall below prior bar low in strong bull trend (urgency)
- Failure of pullback to reach moving average (urgency)
- Failure to reach a magnet → pullback then another attempt

### One-Tick Failed Breakouts

- Most common source of losses for new traders.
- **Why they work:** Smart traders expect stop entries above/below prior bar. If they fade the breakout and volume overwhelms new entries → market reverses. Trapped traders fuel the move.
- **In trading range:** One-tick failed breakout on 5-minute chart is often also one-tick failed breakout on higher time frame.
- **Look for setups in the opposite direction** after a one-tick failure.

### Breakout Tests

- Often test the entry price to the exact tick, trapping out weak hands.
- May miss breakeven stop by one tick → failure to flush = sign of strength.
- **Buy above breakout test bar** if it fails to hit protective stops → bulls aggressively buying, protecting stops.

### Trading Failed Setups

- When a good setup fails, the trapped traders become one-sided fuel for the opposite move.
- **Enter where the trapped traders will have to exit** (at their stop price).
- The move is usually fast and big enough for a scalp because trapped traders drive it.

## 8. Signs of Strength in a Reversal — Ch 2

See [[trends]] for the existing signs of strength checklist. The following extends and specifies reversal context:

### Strong Bull Reversal Signs

| # | Sign |
|---|------|
| 1 | Strong bull reversal bar with large body, small/no tails |
| 2 | Next 2-3 bars also bull bodies ≥ average size |
| 3 | Spike grows to 5-10 bars without pullback > 1 bar |
| 4 | Reverses many bars, swing highs, bear flags of prior trend |
| 5 | One or more bars have low at/1 tick below prior close |
| 6 | One or more bars have open above prior close |
| 7 | One or more bars have close on/near high |
| 8 | Context makes reversal likely (higher low/lower low test after trend line break) |
| 9 | First/second bar closes above highs of many prior bars |
| 10 | First pullback after 3+ bars, lasts 1-2 bars, not strong reversal bar |
| 11 | First pullback doesn't hit breakeven stop |
| 12 | Spike breaks several resistance levels by many ticks |
| 13 | Sense of urgency — want to buy but pullback never comes |
| 14 | Second signal (second attempt to reverse) |
| 15 | Reversal from trend channel line overshoot |
| 16 | Reversing at significant swing high/low |
| 17 | H1/H2 pullbacks have strong bull reversal signal bars |
| 18 | Trending closes, highs, lows, or bodies |
| 19 | Pullbacks small and sideways |
| 20 | Prior breaks of earlier trend lines (not first sign of strength) |
| 21 | Pullback to test extreme lacks momentum (overlapping bars) |
| 22 | Pullback fails at moving average or old trend line |
| 23 | Breakout reverses many recent closes and highs (by close is stronger than by high) |

### Application Rule

- **Strong trend → need strong reversal setup to fade.** Look for as many signs as possible.
- **Two-sided trend → weaker reversal setup acceptable.**
- **Trading range top/bottom → may not even need reversal bar.**
- **Wedge flag → may buy above bear trend bar.**

## 9. Huge Volume Reversals on Daily Charts — Ch 10

- **When stock in steep bear trend has day with volume 5-10× recent average** → bulls may have capitulated, tradable bottom may be present.
- **Huge volume day is very often a gap down day.** If strong bull close → odds of profitable long increase.
- **Look for at least two-legged move to at least the moving average.**
- **Huge volume without bullish price action is NOT enough.** Must see reversal bar or strong bull close.
- **On 1-minute Emini in strong bear trend:** Huge volume bar (~25,000 contracts) unlikely to end bear trend, but often signals pullback coming after one or two lower lows on less volume.

## 10. Always In Direction — Ch 15

See [[core]] for basic always-in concept. Extended treatment:

### Key Rules

- **If forced to be long or short right now, which would you choose?** That's the always-in position.
- **Market is always either always-in long or always-in short.** Usually a general consensus about direction.
- **Determining always-in direction ≠ asking if trend is up or down.** Most trend traders don't hold when market is unclear. Always-in traders must pick a side.
- **Two consecutive reasonably strong trend bars in same direction** probably create new always-in direction.
- **If you're hoping for a pullback to buy → always-in is probably long.**
- **If you're hoping for a rally to short → always-in is probably short.**
- **If you simply cannot decide → market is in a trading range.**

### Trading Rules

- **Always-in is a swing approach.** Look for only the most significant 3-10 swings per day.
- **Most traders exit some/all position** once trade moves 2-3× initial risk.
- **When strong trend transitions into trading range, always-in position often remains unchanged.**
- **In trading range with unchanged always-in direction:** Take scalps only in direction of trend.
- **Don't use always-in in tight trading ranges.** Wait for breakout or failed breakout.
- **On trading range days:** Always-in reversals often unclear until market gets near opposite end of range. Don't buy near top or short near bottom.

### Always-In and Reversals

- **80% of reversal attempts fail.** Until always-in flips, only trade in the always-in direction.
- **If a reversal setup is not strong enough to flip always-in direction → generally not worth trading.**
- **Near flips are great opportunities for with-trend traders.** They correctly believe most reversal attempts fail.

## 11. Opening Reversals — Ch 19

### The Vacuum Effect

- **Institutional traders have orders to fill before open.** They want best price.
- **Gap down + mostly buy orders** → institutions buy immediately if value is great, or wait to buy lower at support.
- **Gap up + mostly sell orders** → institutions wait to short at resistance.
- **Absence of one side creates vacuum** → sharp move to support/resistance → then reversal.

### Opening Range Statistics

- **First bar of day:** ~20% chance of being high or low of day. If strong trend bar → 30%.
- **High or low forms within first 5 bars:** 50% of days.
- **High or low forms within first hour or two:** 90% of days.
- **High usually comes from some kind of double top** (even if not exact).
- **Low usually comes from some kind of double bottom.**

### Opening Patterns

Same as patterns at any other time:
- Trends from the open
- Breakouts and reversals (failed breakouts) at support/resistance
- Breakout pullbacks (failed breakout that failed to reverse)

### Key Support/Resistance on Open

- Moving average
- Trend lines and trend channel lines
- Swing highs/lows (especially yesterday's high/low)
- Breakout areas
- Trading range tops/bottoms
- Measured move targets
- Globex high/low

### Day Type Clues from Opening

- **First bar doji in middle of yesterday's range** → odds of trading range day increase.
- **First hour has many overlapping bars, big tails, multiple reversals** → trading range day likely.
- **Gap + 3-4 strong trend bars with little overlap** → trend day likely.
- **Two consecutive strong trend bars in same direction** → always-in direction established for at least a scalp.

## 12. Gap Openings — Ch 20

### Gap Types

- **Gap from yesterday's close** = most common. Creates support/resistance.
- **Gap from yesterday's high/low** = stronger signal.
- **Gap from Globex** = premarket gap.

### Gap as Magnet

- Market tends to test/fill gaps.
- Gap area becomes support/resistance.
- **Breakout gap:** Strong trend bar at trend start → likely to lead to measured move.
- **Exhaustion gap:** Strong trend bar after protracted trend → likely to lead to correction.
- **Measuring gap:** Pullback doesn't drop below breakout bar high → leads to measured move.

### Gap Trading Rules

- **Large gap in direction of trend** = sign of strength. Look for with-trend entries.
- **Large gap against trend** = potential reversal. Wait for confirmation.
- **Gap fill:** If gap fills quickly → weak signal. If gap holds → strong signal.
- **Gap + reversal bar** = strong setup, especially at support/resistance.

## 13. Time Frames and Chart Types — Ch 13

### Multi-Timeframe Analysis

- **5-minute chart** is the primary trading chart for day trading.
- **1-minute chart** for fine-tuning entries, but too much noise for most traders.
- **15-minute chart** for broader context.
- **60-minute chart** for trend direction.
- **Daily chart** for major support/resistance.

### Time Frame Relationships

- **Micro patterns on 5-minute = patterns on 1-minute.**
- **Patterns on 5-minute = micro patterns on 60-minute.**
- **Reversal on 5-minute may be pullback on 60-minute.**
- **Always trade in direction of higher time frame trend** when possible.

### Chart Types

- **Time-based charts** (5-minute, etc.) = standard. Most Brooks analysis uses these.
- **Tick charts** = number of trades. Useful for very active markets.
- **Volume charts** = number of contracts. Useful for comparing different instruments.
- **Range bars** = fixed point range. Remove time element.

## 14. Key Times of the Day — Ch 11

### Important Time Windows

| Time (ET) | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Open** | Institutional orders creating vacuum effects |
| **First 30 min** | Opening range formation, high/low of day often set |
| **First hour** | 90% of days have high or low formed |
| **10:00-10:30** | Often reversal time after opening move |
| **11:00-11:30** | Lunchtime lull begins, trading range likely |
| **12:00-1:00** | Lunch hour, low volume, tight ranges |
| **1:00-2:00** | Market begins to move again |
| **2:00-3:00** | Trend resumption or reversal |
| **3:00-3:30** | Late-day momentum often begins |
| **3:30-4:00** | Final push, institutional rebalancing |

### Trading Rules by Time

- **First hour:** Most volatile, most reversals, but also most false signals. Wait for clear setups.
- **Midday:** Lower volume, tighter ranges. Fade extremes or wait for breakout.
- **Afternoon:** Trend resumption common. If morning was trending, look for continuation.
- **Last 30 minutes:** Institutional activity. Can be very strong moves. Don't fight the close.

## 15. Markets — Ch 12

### Market Characteristics

- **Emini (ES):** Most liquid, best for day trading. 1 tick = 0.25 points = $12.50.
- **SPY:** ETF, less liquid than ES. 1 penny = $1 (if 100 shares).
- **Stocks:** Individual stocks have personalities. Same traders every day create patterns.
- **Bonds:** Less volatile, larger point values. Good for swing trading.
- **Forex:** 24-hour market. Best during London/NY overlap.

### Market Selection

- **Trade the most liquid market** for your style.
- **Day traders:** ES, NQ, YM, or liquid stocks.
- **Swing traders:** Stocks, ETFs, or futures.
- **The patterns are the same** across all markets. Only scale changes.

## 16. Extreme Scalping — Ch 16

### Definition

- Taking many small profits throughout the day.
- Usually 1-2 point targets in ES.
- Very high win rate (70%+) but small reward.
- Requires constant attention and quick decisions.

### When to Extreme Scalp

- Trading range days.
- Tight ranges within trends.
- When you can't commit to swing trades.
- When the market is unclear but you want to stay active.

### Rules

- **Only scalp in direction of always-in** when possible.
- **Use limit orders** at support/resistance.
- **Take profits quickly** — don't let scalp become swing against you.
- **Move stop to breakeven** after first target hit.
- **If market breaks out of range, switch to swing trading.**

## 17. Detailed Day Trading Examples — Ch 21

### Example Patterns

- **Spike and channel bull day:** Strong opening spike, then channel. Buy pullbacks in channel.
- **Spike and channel bear day:** Strong opening spike down, then channel. Short rallies in channel.
- **Trading range day:** Market opens in range, stays in range. Fade extremes.
- **Trending trading range day:** Market trends but with large pullbacks forming ranges within trend.
- **Double top/bottom day:** Market tests yesterday's high/low, forms double top/bottom, reverses.
- **Trend resumption day:** Morning trend, midday correction, afternoon resumption.

### Trading Rules

- **Always classify the day type** early. It determines your strategy.
- **Trend days:** Trade with trend only. Don't fade.
- **Trading range days:** Fade extremes. Don't chase breakouts.
- **Mixed days:** Adapt. Trade what the market gives you.

## 18. Daily, Weekly, and Monthly Charts — Ch 22

### Higher Timeframe Analysis

- **Daily charts** show major trends and support/resistance.
- **Weekly charts** show long-term trends. Good for position trading.
- **Monthly charts** show secular trends. Good for investing.
- **Patterns are the same** on all timeframes. Only scale changes.

### Trading Higher Timeframes

- **Daily chart traders** look for setups that take days to develop.
- **Weekly chart traders** look for setups that take weeks.
- **Monthly chart traders** look for setups that take months.
- **All use the same price action principles.**

### Reversals on Higher Timeframes

- **Major trend reversals on daily charts** can lead to moves lasting weeks or months.
- **Climactic reversals on daily charts** can be very profitable but rare.
- **Wedges on daily charts** provide excellent entry points for swing trades.
- **Always check higher timeframe** for context before entering a trade.

## 19. Options — Ch 23

### Options and Price Action

- **Options are derivatives.** Their price is based on the underlying asset.
- **Price action on the underlying** determines option direction.
- **Use the same price action principles** for options trading.
- **Options add time decay** — trades must move in your favor before expiration.

### Options Strategies

- **Buying calls/puts:** Simple directional bet. Use price action for entry.
- **Spreads:** Limit risk but also limit reward. Good for range-bound markets.
- **Straddles/strangles:** Bet on volatility, not direction. Use before big events.
- **Covered calls/puts:** Income strategy. Use price action to time entries.

### Options and Reversals

- **Reversal patterns on the underlying** = potential options entries.
- **Buy calls at bull reversal bottoms.**
- **Buy puts at bear reversal tops.**
- **Use the same stop placement** as the underlying (adjust for option delta).

## 20. The Best Trades — Ch 24

### Characteristics of Best Trades

- **Clear setup** with multiple confirmations.
- **Strong signal bar** with small tails.
- **Good context** (trend, support/resistance, multiple timeframes).
- **Favorable risk/reward** (at least 2:1).
- **High probability** (60%+ for swing trades).

### Best Reversal Trades

- **Major trend reversal** after strong trend line break.
- **Climactic reversal** after consecutive climaxes.
- **Wedge reversal** at trend channel line overshoot.
- **Final flag reversal** after protracted trend.
- **Double top/bottom pullback** at significant support/resistance.

### Trade Management

- **Enter on stop** 1 tick beyond signal bar.
- **Stop** 1 tick beyond signal bar extreme.
- **Target** at least 2× risk for swing trades.
- **Scale out** partial position at 1× risk.
- **Trail stop** on remainder.
- **Exit before major news events** if in profit.

## 21. Trading Guidelines — Ch 25

### Core Guidelines

1. **Trade with the trend** until there is a clear reversal.
2. **Wait for the close** before entering on a signal bar.
3. **Don't trade in the first 5-10 minutes** unless you see a very strong setup.
4. **Use the 5-minute chart** as your primary chart.
5. **Look for 3-10 good trades per day**, not 20 mediocre ones.
6. **Take partial profits** at 1× risk.
7. **Move stop to breakeven** after partial profit taken.
8. **Don't revenge trade** after a loss.
9. **Don't overtrade** during slow periods.
10. **Always know your always-in direction.**

### Reversal-Specific Guidelines

1. **Don't look for reversals in strong trends.** Most reversal attempts fail.
2. **Wait for a trend line break** before considering countertrend trades.
3. **After the trend line break, wait for a test** of the trend extreme.
4. **Take countertrend trade only if reversal setup develops** during the test.
5. **Expect at least two legs** in the countertrend move.
6. **If the test is too strong (tight channel, no pullbacks), the trend has likely resumed.** Start over.
7. **The strongest reversals come from the weakest trends.** Look for two-sided trading.
8. **Always check the higher timeframe** for context.
9. **Don't fight the close.** If the market is closing strongly against your position, exit.
10. **When in doubt, stay out.**

## Summary

Book 3 provides the reversal-specific knowledge that complements the universal concepts in [[core]], the trend-specific knowledge in [[trends]], and the range-specific knowledge in [[ranges]]. The key insights are:

1. **Reversals are events, not states.** They occur at specific moments when the market transitions between conditions.
2. **Most reversal attempts fail.** Markets have inertia. Only trade reversals with clear confirmation.
3. **Every reversal is a failed breakout** of something (trend line, channel, flag, swing extreme).
4. **Five main reversal patterns:** Major Trend Reversal, Climactic Reversal, Wedge Reversal, Expanding Triangle, Final Flag.
5. **Always-in direction is the key filter.** Don't trade countertrend until always-in flips.
6. **The strongest reversals come after the weakest trends** (two-sided trading, multiple failed attempts).
7. **Opening reversals are common** (90% of days have high/low in first hour).
8. **Higher timeframe context matters.** Always check daily/60-minute charts.
9. **Trade management is the same** for reversal trades as for any other trade.
10. **When in doubt, stay out.** The market will always give you another chance.
