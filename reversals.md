# Reversals — Al Brooks Framework

> Al Brooks, "Trading Price Action Reversals" (2012, Wiley). Load ONLY when reversal signals are detected or being evaluated. For universal concepts (bar anatomy, bar counting, breakouts, 20-EMA, close importance, two legs, risk management, trade management, time frames, glossary), see [[core]]. For trend-specific knowledge, see [[trends]]. For range-specific knowledge, see [[ranges]]. Brooks terminology only.

---

## Quick Reference

- **Reversals are events, not states.** They occur at specific moments when the market transitions between conditions.
- **Four requirements for MTR:** (1) existing trend, (2) countertrend move breaking trend line, (3) test of extreme, (4) second reversal with consensus.
- **Most reversal attempts fail (~80%).** Markets have inertia. Trade with-trend until reversal is confirmed.
- **Every reversal is a failed breakout** of something (trend line, channel, flag, swing extreme).
- **Consecutive climaxes increase reversal odds.** Two → watch. Three → 60%+ chance of 10-bar, two-legged correction.
- **Wedges are three-push patterns.** Third push often overshoots trend channel line.
- **Final flags = last flag before reversal.** Every reversal follows some kind of flag.
- **Always-in direction matters.** Don't trade countertrend until always-in flips. See [[core]] for always-in concept.

## 1. Major Trend Reversal (MTR) — Ch 3

The core reversal pattern. Two trends on the chart with a reversal in between — bull trend reversed into bear trend, or vice versa.

### Four Requirements

| # | Requirement | Details |
|---|------------|---------|
| 1 | **Existing trend** | Clear trend on the chart in front of you |
| 2 | **Countertrend move** | Strong enough to break the trend line, usually beyond the moving average |
| 3 | **Test of extreme** | Higher high, double top, or lower high at bull top; lower low, double bottom, or higher low at bear bottom |
| 4 | **Consensus** | Second reversal goes far enough for traders to agree trend has reversed |

### Key Rules

- **Every MTR begins with a trend line break or trend channel line overshoot and reversal.** All eventually break the trend line.
- **The trend line break does not reverse the trend.** It's the first significant sign that countertrend traders are getting strong enough to trade against.
- **After the trend line break, there will be a test of the old trend extreme.** The test can slightly overshoot or undershoot.
- **Take a countertrend trade only if a reversal setup develops during this test.** Expect at least two legs.
- **True V bottoms/tops without trend channel line overshoots are too rare to consider.** Focus on common patterns.
- **If the test goes too far in too tight a channel, don't look for a reversal.** Trend has likely resumed. Start over.

### Test Channel Assessment

| Channel Strength | Implication |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Tight, steep, no pullbacks** | Trend likely resuming — don't fade |
| **Many overlapping bars, bear bodies, wedge shape** | Test likely to fail → reversal |
| **Noticeably less slope than original trend** | Countertrend traders gaining control |

### Overshoots vs Undershoots

- **Overshoot** (higher high after bear break, lower low after bull break) = final leg of old trend. Most extreme price.
- **Undershoot** (higher low in new bull trend, lower high in new bear trend) = pullback from first leg of new trend.
- **Tests are frequently two legs** (e.g., two-legged rally after bear breakout).

### Probability

- **Average setup:** 40% profitable swing, 30% small loss, 30% small profit. Still profitable overall.
- **Best setups:** 60%+ profitable swing.
- **Most MTRs lead to trading ranges, not opposite trends (~70%).** Still tradable with large countertrend swings.

### Head and Shouldars Context

- Move down from head usually breaks below the bull trend line for entire trend.
- Right shoulder = lower high breakout pullback short setup.
- Neckline = horizontal line across lows. Break below = bears holding shorts, expecting measured move down.
- If sell-off from head to neckline is weak → entire pattern is just a large bull flag.

## 2. Climactic Reversals — Ch 4

A spike followed by a spike in the opposite direction. The market is always trying to break out, then trying to make every breakout fail.

### Definition

- **Climax:** Any unsustainable behavior — trend bar or series of trend bars with relatively large ranges and little overlap.
- **Climactic reversal:** A climax soon followed by a sharp move in the opposite direction. All are failed breakouts of something.
- **Two-bar reversal on higher time frame.** The spikes can contain many bars on your chart, but there's always a higher time frame where it's a simple two-bar reversal.

### Vacuum Effect

- Strong bears wait to short until market reaches a level where they see value (e.g., above trend channel line).
- Strong bulls wait to take profits until market reaches a level where they think it won't go higher.
- **Absence of selling creates a vacuum** that sucks the market to the level where bears will short and bulls will take profits.
- Once both sides sell aggressively → sharp reversal.

### Consecutive Climaxes

| Climaxes | Odds of Correction | Expected Correction |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Moderate | May resume after brief pause |
| 2 | Higher | Watch for reversal setup |
| 3+ | 60%+ | At least 10-bar, two-legged correction |

- **Two consecutive buy climaxes** = effectively a double top (two pushes up).
- **Three consecutive buy climaxes** = effectively a wedge top. Can look like a bull spike, but same implications.
- **Pause between climaxes** can be any bar that is not another big trend bar (doji, bear body, small bar).

### Trading Rules

- **Only very experienced traders should short buy climaxes.** Easy to misread and short a strong bull.
- **After a sell climax:** Bulls take profits, bears initiate shorts, both expect lower prices for 10+ bars and two legs.
- **Smart traders fade likely climaxes:** Short the close of the strong bull bar, above its high, at the close of the next bar or two.
- **Weak traders do the opposite:** Weak bulls buy in panic, weak bears cover shorts — both trading on emotion against computers.

### After the Climax

- **Big up + big down = big confusion = trading range** (at least for a while).
- If bulls win → spike and channel bull trend. If bears win → spike and channel bear trend.

### Parabolic Moves

- Slope is curved and increasing (not linear). All are trend channel line overshoots and reversals.
- Often have three pushes = wedge tops.
- Best reversals have large reversal bars and preferably a second entry.
- First leg of reversal will almost always break the trend line.

## 3. Wedges and Three-Push Reversal Patterns — Ch 5

When the market makes three pushes in one direction and then creates a reversal setup, first target is the beginning of the pattern, next is a measured move based on pattern height.

### Key Principle

All three-push patterns are the same underlying behavior:
- Wedge, micro wedge, parabolic wedge, wedge with fourth push
- Other triangles (including expanding)
- Triple top/bottom, double top/bottom pullback, head and shoulders
- Failed breakout below/above double top/bottom

**A third consecutive reversal is usually enough for trend traders to give up and reversal traders to gain control.**

### Wedge Pullbacks vs Reversals

| Aspect | Wedge Pullback | Wedge Reversal |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Direction | With-trend | Countertrend |
| Entry | First signal OK | Wait for second signal |
| Size | Usually 10-20 bars | Usually 20+ bars |
| Shape | Often subtle | Needs clear trend channel line |
| Context | In established trend | At end of trend |

### Wedge Shape

- **Trend line slope > trend channel line slope** = urgency. Bulls buying on smaller pullbacks, bears exiting on smaller sell-offs.
- Converging lines = classic wedge. Diverging = expanding triangle. Parallel = stairs. **All trade the same.**

### Trading Rules

- **Overshoot of trend channel line increases odds of success.**
- **If wedge reversal triggers but fails** (market extends beyond extreme) → often runs quickly to measured move based on wedge height.
- **Fourth push:** Sometimes market resets count. Stronger momentum on second push = more likely reset.
- **Three pushes alone, especially small, rarely reverse a trend** without prior trend line break or major trend channel overshoot.

### Shrinking Stairs

- Each breakout extends less than prior → waning momentum.
- Increases odds of profitable countertrend trade.
- Usually signals imminent two-legged pullback.

### Micro Wedge

- Three consecutive bars or three out of four/five consecutive bars.
- Usually results in only a minor correction.
- **In strong trend, don't trade micro wedge reversals.** Only in trading ranges or near extremes.

### Opening Wedges

- Wedges are often opening reversal patterns in the first hour.
- **Never look for reversal from steep, tight wedge after first hour** — except as opening reversal.

## 4. Expanding Triangles — Ch 6

Reversal or continuation pattern with at least five swings (sometimes seven, rarely nine), each greater than the prior. Traps traders on each new breakout.

### Structure

- **Expanding triangle bottom:** Rallies above last higher high (trapping longs in), collapses to third low (trapping longs out, bears in), reverses up forcing both sides to chase.
- **Expanding triangle top:** Opposite pattern.
- **Final reversal always follows a strong leg** that breaks some significant trend line → variant of MTR.
- **Third push down = lower low MTR buy setup.**

### Trading

- **First objective:** Breakout beyond opposite side of triangle.
- **If breakout succeeds:** Measured move ≈ height of last leg.
- **If breakout fails:** Pattern becomes continuation (expanding triangle flag).
- **Usually five turns before reversal**, sometimes seven. Each leg = valid scalp.
- **Once fifth leg complete, swing part of position.**

## 5. Final Flags — Ch 7

Every reversal follows some kind of flag. A final flag is the last flag before the trend reverses.

### Characteristics

| # | Characteristic |
|---|---------------|
| 1 | Occurs in trend that has gone on for dozens of bars |
| 2 | Mostly horizontal with signs of two-sided trading |
| 3 | Several trend bars in opposite direction |
| 4 | Bars with prominent tails |
| 5 | Several reversals within range |
| 6 | Bars overlap previous bar by ≥50% |
| 7 | Can be as small as one or two bars (after exhaustive climax) |
| 8 | Tight trading ranges often become final flags |

### How Final Flags Work

- Traders see market is overdone, expect correction soon.
- Enter on breakout expecting only a scalp, not a swing.
- With everyone taking small profits, breakout quickly reverses.
- **Reversal can lead to:** pullback, larger correction (trading range), or trend reversal.
- **Trend reversal more likely if:** strong bear leg in flag broke well below bull trend line, or earlier selling pressure.

### One-Bar Final Flags

- After consecutive climaxes, a pause bar (doji, small trend bar) can become the final flag.
- If next bar or two is a large spike in trend direction and then reverses → pause bar was one-bar final flag.
- Usually results in two-legged pullback lasting 10+ bars.

### Final Flag as Double Top/Bottom

- Push up just before flag = first push. Upside breakout = test of high = second push.
- If more sellers than buyers on test → reverses down.
- **Same underlying force as double top/bottom**, but looks different enough for separate classification.

## 6. Double Top and Bottom Pullbacks — Ch 8

Reversal pattern (not continuation) where market forms double bottom, rallies, then has higher low pullback testing just above the double bottom low.

### Structure

- **Double bottom pullback long:** Double bottom → rally → higher low pullback → buy.
- **Double top pullback short:** Double top → decline → lower high pullback → sell.
- **Can be thought of as three-push pattern** where third push didn't have strong-enough sellers to create new low.
- **Second bottom often slightly lower than first** — common and actually preferable.

### Key Rules

- **Reversal pattern, not continuation** (unlike double bottom bull flag).
- **If second bottom doesn't reach first** → risk of only two-legged correction → better to scalp.
- **When shape is good and signal bars are strong** → one of the most reliable buy setups.
- **Move up from second bottom is usually a breakout** → pullback is breakout pullback (most reliable setup).
- **Two failed attempts to exceed prior extreme** → usually leads to reversal.

### Variations

- Triple bottom, triangle, head and shoulders, final flag, double bottom pullback — **significance is the same.**
- Use loose definitions. When something resembles a reliable pattern, it will trade like it.

## 7. Failures — Ch 9

Every failed setup (~40% of the time) often creates an excellent setup in the opposite direction. The trapped traders become one-sided fuel.

### Common Failures That Lead to Opposite Trades

- Failed H1, H2, L1, or L2
- Failed breakout of anything (swing high/low, trend line, trend channel line)
- Failure to break through a magnet (measured move, prior swing, trend line)
- Failed final flag (reversal attempt fails → breakout pullback)
- Failed wedge
- Failed signal bar or entry bar
- Failed one-tick breakout
- Failed reversal (failed double bottom/top, failed head and shoulders)
- Failure to reach profit target (5, 9, 17 tick failures in Eminis)

### Failures That Are Signs of Strong Trend

- Failure to fall below prior bar low in strong bull trend (urgency)
- Failure of pullback to reach moving average (urgency)
- Failure to reach a magnet → pullback then another attempt

### One-Tick Failed Breakouts

- Most common source of losses for new traders.
- **Why they work:** Smart traders expect stop entries above/below prior bar. If they fade the breakout and volume overwhelms new entries → market reverses. Trapped traders fuel the move.
- **Look for setups in the opposite direction** after a one-tick failure.

### Breakout Tests

- Often test entry price to the exact tick, trapping out weak hands.
- May miss breakeven stop by one tick → failure to flush = sign of strength.
- **Buy above breakout test bar** if it fails to hit protective stops → bulls aggressively buying, protecting stops.

See [[trends]] §2 for the existing signs of strength checklist (22 signs of strong trend, strong breakout signs, strong reversal signs). The reversal-specific extension:

### Application Rule

- **Strong trend → need strong reversal setup to fade.** Look for as many signs as possible from [[trends]] §2.
- **Two-sided trend → weaker reversal setup acceptable.**
- **Trading range top/bottom → may not even need reversal bar.**
- **Wedge flag → may buy above bear trend bar.**
- **The stronger the trend, the more signs of reversal strength needed.**

## 9. Huge Volume Reversals on Daily Charts — Ch 10

- **Stock in steep bear trend + day with volume 5-10× recent average** → bulls may have capitulated, tradable bottom possible.
- **Huge volume day is often a gap down day.** If strong bull close → odds of profitable long increase.
- **Look for at least two-legged move to at least the moving average.**
- **Huge volume without bullish price action is NOT enough.** Must see reversal bar or strong bull close.

## 10. Always In — Extended — Ch 15

See [[core]] for the basic always-in concept. Extended day trading application:

### Key Rules

- **Two consecutive reasonably strong trend bars in same direction** probably create new always-in direction.
- **If you're hoping for a pullback to buy → always-in is probably long.**
- **If you're hoping for a rally to short → always-in is probably short.**
- **If you simply cannot decide → market is in a trading range.**

### Trading Rules

- **Always-in is a swing approach.** Look for only 3-10 significant swings per day.
- **Exit some/all once trade moves 2-3× initial risk.**
- **When strong trend transitions to trading range, always-in often unchanged.**
- **In trading range with unchanged always-in:** Take scalps only in trend direction.
- **Don't use always-in in tight trading ranges.** Wait for breakout or failed breakout.
- **On trading range days:** Always-in reversals often unclear until near opposite end of range. Don't buy near top or short near bottom.

### Always-In and Reversals

- **80% of reversal attempts fail.** Until always-in flips, only trade in always-in direction.
- **If reversal setup not strong enough to flip always-in → generally not worth trading.**
- **Near flips are great opportunities for with-trend traders.** Most reversal attempts fail.

## 11. Opening Reversals — Ch 19

### The Vacuum Effect

- **Institutional traders have orders to fill before open.** They want best price.
- **Gap down + mostly buy orders** → institutions buy immediately if value is great, or wait to buy lower at support.
- **Gap up + mostly sell orders** → institutions wait to short at resistance.
- **Absence of one side creates vacuum** → sharp move to support/resistance → then reversal.

### Opening Range Statistics

| Stat | Value |
|------|-------|
| First bar = high or low of day | ~20% (30% if strong trend bar) |
| High or low within first 5 bars | 50% of days |
| High or low within first hour | 90% of days |
| High usually from | Some kind of double top |
| Low usually from | Some kind of double bottom |

### Day Type Clues from Opening

- **First bar doji in middle of yesterday's range** → trading range day likely.
- **First hour: many overlapping bars, big tails, multiple reversals** → trading range day likely.
- **Gap + 3-4 strong trend bars with little overlap** → trend day likely.
- **Two consecutive strong trend bars in same direction** → always-in direction established for at least a scalp.

### Key Support/Resistance on Open

- Moving average, trend lines, trend channel lines
- Swing highs/lows (especially yesterday's high/low)
- Breakout areas, trading range tops/bottoms
- Measured move targets, Globex high/low

## 12. Gap Openings — Ch 20

### Gap as Magnet

- Market tends to test/fill gaps. Gap area becomes support/resistance.
- **Breakout gap:** Strong trend bar at trend start → likely measured move.
- **Exhaustion gap:** Strong trend bar after protracted trend → likely correction.
- **Measuring gap:** Pullback doesn't drop below breakout bar high → measured move.

### Trading Rules

- **Large gap in direction of trend** = sign of strength. Look for with-trend entries.
- **Large gap against trend** = potential reversal. Wait for confirmation.
- **Gap fill quickly** = weak signal. **Gap holds** = strong signal.
- **Gap + reversal bar** = strong setup, especially at support/resistance.

## 13. Key Times of the Day — Ch 11

| Time (ET) | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Open** | Institutional orders creating vacuum effects |
| **First 30 min** | Opening range formation, high/low of day often set |
| **First hour** | 90% of days have high or low formed |
| **10:00-10:30** | Often reversal time after opening move |
| **11:00-11:30** | Lunchtime lull begins, trading range likely |
| **12:00-1:00** | Lunch hour, low volume, tight ranges |
| **1:00-2:00** | Market begins to move again |
| **2:00-3:00** | Trend resumption or reversal |
| **3:00-3:30** | Late-day momentum often begins |
| **3:30-4:00** | Final push, institutional rebalancing |

### Trading Rules by Time

- **First hour:** Most volatile, most reversals, most false signals. Wait for clear setups.
- **Midday:** Lower volume, tighter ranges. Fade extremes or wait for breakout.
- **Afternoon:** Trend resumption common. If morning was trending, look for continuation.
- **Last 30 min:** Institutional activity. Can be very strong. Don't fight the close.

## 14. Extreme Scalping — Ch 16

- Taking many small profits throughout the day. Usually 1-2 point targets in ES.
- Very high win rate (70%+) but small reward. Requires constant attention.
- **When to scalp:** Trading range days, tight ranges within trends, when market is unclear.
- **Only scalp in direction of always-in** when possible.
- **Use limit orders** at support/resistance. Take profits quickly.
- **If market breaks out of range, switch to swing trading.**

### Multi-Timeframe Context

- Patterns are the same on all timeframes. Only scale changes.
- **Major MTR on 1D chart** can lead to moves lasting weeks or months.
- **Always check higher timeframe** for context before entering a trade.
- **Reversal on 5-minute may be pullback on 60-minute.** See [[core]] for time frame relationships.

## 16. Best Reversal Trades — Ch 24

### Characteristics

- Clear setup with multiple confirmations.
- Strong signal bar with small tails.
- Good context (trend, support/resistance, multiple timeframes).
- Favorable risk/reward (at least 2:1).
- High probability (60%+ for swing trades).

### Top Reversal Setups

1. **Major trend reversal** after strong trend line break
2. **Climactic reversal** after consecutive climaxes
3. **Wedge reversal** at trend channel line overshoot
4. **Final flag reversal** after protracted trend
5. **Double top/bottom pullback** at significant support/resistance

### Reversal-Specific Trade Management

- **Enter on stop** 1 tick beyond signal bar.
- **Stop** 1 tick beyond signal bar extreme.
- **Target** at least 2× risk for swing trades.
- **Scale out** partial position at 1× risk.
- **Trail stop** on remainder.
- **Exit before major news events** if in profit.
- See [[core]] for universal trade management rules.

## 17. Reversal Trading Guidelines — Ch 25

1. **Don't look for reversals in strong trends.** Most reversal attempts fail.
2. **Wait for a trend line break** before considering countertrend trades.
3. **After the trend line break, wait for a test** of the trend extreme.
4. **Take countertrend trade only if reversal setup develops** during the test.
5. **Expect at least two legs** in the countertrend move.
6. **If the test is too strong (tight channel, no pullbacks), trend has likely resumed.** Start over.
7. **The strongest reversals come from the weakest trends.** Look for two-sided trading.
8. **Always check the higher timeframe** for context.
9. **Don't fight the close.** If market is closing strongly against your position, exit.
10. **When in doubt, stay out.** The market will always give you another chance.

## Summary

Reversals are event overlays that load alongside the state module (core + trends or core + ranges). The five main reversal patterns are:

1. **Major Trend Reversal** — trend line break → test of extreme → reversal
2. **Climactic Reversal** — spike → opposite spike (failed breakout)
3. **Wedge Reversal** — three pushes → overshoot → reversal
4. **Expanding Triangle** — five+ swings, each greater → reversal
5. **Final Flag** — last flag in trend → breakout fails → reversal

All share the same underlying structure: existing trend → countertrend strength → test → confirmation. Most attempts fail (~80%). Only trade with clear confirmation and favorable trader's equation.
