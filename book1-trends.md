# Book 1: Trading Price Action Trends — Compact Reference

> Al Brooks, "Trading Price Action Trends" (2011, Wiley). Brooks terminology only.

## Quick Reference

- **Trend is king.** Always classify trend vs TR first. With-trend entries = highest probability.
- **Weak signals in strong trends = FEATURE**, not a warning.
- **Most climaxes → trading ranges**, not reversals.
- **Trader's Equation:** prob × reward > (1 − prob) × risk.
- **Two-legged principle:** market tries to do something twice.
- **The close matters most.** Every bar, every time frame. Don't enter before the bar closes.

---

## 1. Trend Classification

| Type | Structure | MA Alignment | Bars | Pullbacks |
|------|-----------|-------------|------|-----------|
| **Bull trend** | HH + HL | 20-EMA > SMA50, price above 20-EMA | Majority bull trend bars (body ≥60%) | Shallow, 1-2 legs |
| **Bear trend** | LH + LL | Reverse | Majority bear trend bars | Shallow |
| **Trading range** | Overlapping | 20-EMA flat | Alternating direction | N/A — fade extremes |

**Spectrum of price action.** The market exists on a spectrum from extreme trends to extreme trading ranges. A trend bar and a doji bar are the two extremes of a single bar; trends and trading ranges are the two extremes of the market. Any chart segment can be classified as trending (one side dominant) or two-sided (alternating control).

**Market inertia.** Markets resist change. Almost every reversal attempt in a strong trend ends up as a flag, and the trend resumes. Successive flags get larger as bulls take profits at new highs and bears become more aggressive. Eventually the bears overwhelm the bulls and a trading range breaks to the downside — but this usually happens after several failed reversal attempts.

**Always-in direction.** If forced to be long or short right now, which would you choose? Most traders should only trade in the always-in direction. A strong trend bar can flip the always-in direction in a single bar.

## 2. Bar Anatomy

| Type | Definition | Key |
|------|-----------|-----|
| **Trend bar** | Body ≥60% range | Strong = 70%+, small tails, close in top/bottom 25% |
| **Doji** | Body <40% range | Equilibrium, indecision |
| **Reversal bar** | Tail 1/3–1/2 of bar + body in reversal direction | On strong trend days: best-looking reversal bars are **countertrend traps** |
| **Outside bar** | High > prior high AND low < prior low | Close direction = bias (in TR) |
| **Inside bar** | High AND low inside prior bar | ii/iii = coiling; ioi = breakout pending |

**Signal bar** = last bar of setup (identified after close). **Entry bar** = bar where order fills (1 tick beyond signal bar). **Follow-through bar** = bar after entry — strength matters. **The CLOSE matters most.**

### Reversal Bars — Detailed

Best bull reversal bar has: open near/below prior close, close above open AND above prior close, lower tail 1/3–1/2 bar height, small upper tail, little overlap with prior bars, strong entry bar follows, close reverses closes/highs of more than one bar.

Best bear reversal bar has: open near/above prior close, close well below prior close, upper tail 1/3–1/2 bar height, small lower tail, little overlap with prior bars, strong entry bar follows, close reverses closes/lows of more than one bar.

**Key rules:**
- A reversal bar that largely overlaps prior bars is part of a trading range — nothing to reverse, do not use as signal.
- A large doji is a one-bar trading range — don't buy at top or sell at bottom of it.
- If a bull reversal bar has a large upper tail, countertrend conviction faded — take only if body is strong and context supports (e.g., second entry).
- In a strong trend, reversal bars often fail in the final seconds before close — trapped traders fuel continuation.
- The bar after the signal bar matters: a doji inside bar after a signal = lack of urgency, consider not taking the trade.

### Other Signal Bar Types

**Two-bar reversal:** Consecutive trend bars of ~equal size in opposite directions. Bull bottom = bear bar then bull bar. Bear top = bull bar then bear bar. One of the most common and important reversal setups. Strongest moves usually begin with a reversal bar or two-bar reversal.

**Three-bar reversal:** Variation where middle bar is small/doji. Corresponds to a 15-min reversal bar when the third bar closes at the same time as the 15-min bar.

**ii pattern:** Two consecutive inside bars, second inside the first. Coiling/breakout mode. Countertrend breakout from ii after protracted move often leads to large reversal. Stop = beyond opposite side of both bars.

**ioi pattern:** Inside-outside-inside. Breakout mode — enter in direction of breakout of second inside bar, if context supports.

**Small bars:** A small bar near the high/low of a big bar or TR can set up a reversal, especially if body is in trade direction. In a trend, small bars in pullbacks are with-trend setups only.

### Outside Bars — Detailed

Every outside bar is part of a trading range by definition (total overlap of prior bar). Usually best NOT to enter on breakout of outside bar — risk too large.

**Valid outside bar entries:**
- Outside bar where bar before it was a good signal bar (the outside bar acts as entry bar).
- Outside bar as second entry after trend line break or trend channel line overshoot.
- ioi pattern at a swing extreme with small inside bar near outside bar's high/low.

**With-trend outside bars in first leg of reversal:** Function like strong trend bars, not TR bars. Trapped traders on wrong side create powerful moves — e.g., bears short below prior bar low, bar reverses up into outside up bar, trapping bears. Two legs expected from the bottom of the outside bar.

## 3. Bar Counting: H1/H2/H3/H4 / L1/L2/L3/L4

Pullback counting within a single leg. Resets on new leg.

| Count | Meaning | Reliability | Action |
|-------|---------|-------------|--------|
| H1/L1 | First pullback | Lower | Early entry in spike phase only |
| **H2/L2** | **Second pullback** | **Standard** | **Primary with-trend entry (M2B/M2S near 20-EMA)** |
| H3/L3 | Third push | Wedge potential | Often exhaustion — potential fade |
| H4/L4 | Fourth push | Context-dependent | Possible reversal |

**H2/L2 = ABC correction.** First leg = A, the H1/L1 = B, final leg = C. Breakout from C is the H2/L2 entry bar.

**H3/L3 = wedge flag.** When a bear pullback ends after a third leg, it's a low 3 sell setup = wedge bear flag. Same for bull side.

**Edge cases:**
- Low 1 in strong bull trend → NOT VALID (Brooks: "Incorrect to use term low 1 in strong bull trend")
- Failed H2 → look for H3 as wedge top
- Context > count number always

## 4. The Importance of the Close

**Never enter before the bar closes.** Once or twice every day, the signal you thought was forming collapses in the final seconds. You need ~8 early entries to work for every 1 that fails — that ratio won't happen.

**Common traps:**
- **Trapped in:** Bull reversal bar forming nicely in a bear trend, looks strong at minute 3, collapses to close on its low in the final seconds. Early longs lose 8+ ticks.
- **Trapped out:** Trade has 3-5 ticks open profit, 3-min chart shows bear reversal bar forming, you tighten stop, market drops to tick your stop then pops up. You missed the profit.

**The close is what institutions trade on.** Many institutional traders use line charts (based on close only). The close is the only price they consider — this increases its importance.

**Pay attention to strong closes.** If the entry bar's body suddenly increases in the final seconds (e.g., from 2-tick to 4-tick body), reduce scalp portion, swing more contracts. Strong closes on follow-through bars mean the same.

## 5. Two Legs

The market regularly tries to do something twice. All moves tend to subdivide into two smaller moves. If it fails in two attempts, it usually tries the opposite.

**Every trend line break** is the start of a new leg in the opposite direction. Any breakout, major reversal, or capitulation will usually have at least a two-legged move.

**Three-push patterns overlap with two legs.** Sometimes one leg of a two-push move is composed of two smaller legs, creating three pushes total. Two of the pushes may be part of a single leg comparable in strength to the other single-move leg.

> **"Once you realize strong trend, you don't need a setup to enter."** — Brooks

### Additional Signs: Strong Bull Breakout

- Large bull trend body, small/no tails. Larger bar = more likely to succeed.
- Spike goes very far, lasts several bars, breaks several resistance levels (MA, prior swing highs, trend lines) by many ticks.
- 5–10 bars without pulling back more than a bar or so.
- Bars with lows at or 1 tick below prior close.
- Bars with opens above prior close.
- Bars with closes on/near their highs.
- First pullback only after 3+ bars, lasts 1-2 bars, doesn't reach breakout point.
- Breakout reverses many recent closes and highs (5–20+ bars).

### Additional Signs: Strong Bear Breakout

Mirror of bull breakout signs: large bear body, small/no tails, breaks several support levels, 5–10 bars without pullback, bars with highs at/above prior close, opens below prior close, closes on/near lows.

### Additional Signs: Strong Bull Reversal

- Strong bull reversal bar with large body, small/no tails.
- Next 2–3 bars also bull bodies ≥ average size.
- Spike grows to 5–10 bars, reverses many bars/swing highs/bear flags of prior trend.
- Second attempt to reverse (second signal).
- Reversal from trend channel line overshoot.
- Prior breaks of bear trend lines (not first sign of bullish strength).
- Pullback to test bear low lacks momentum (overlapping bars, bull bodies).
- Pullback fails at MA or old bear trend line.

### Additional Signs: Strong Bear Reversal

Mirror of bull reversal signs.

## 7. Entry Types

| Type | Entry | Stop | Target |
|------|-------|------|--------|
| **H2/L2 at 20-EMA** | 1 tick beyond signal bar | 1 tick beyond signal bar extreme | Measured move of prior leg |
| **Spike & channel** | 1 tick above/below channel line break | Opposite side of channel | Scalp + trail |
| **1st breakout pullback** | 1 tick beyond signal bar | 1 tick beyond signal bar | Measured move of breakout |
| **Climactic reversal (2-stage)** | 1 tick beyond pause bar after climax | Beyond climax extreme | Measured move + prior swing |
| **Late entry** | At market (swing portion only) | Same stop as original entry | Same target as original |
| **Second entry** | Same as first, 2nd attempt | Same as first | Same as first (more reliable) |

**Countertrend entries** (lower probability): only when second signal, climax + opposite breakout, or failed wedge. Generally avoid.

### Stop Entries vs Limit Entries

**Stop entries** (standard): Buy 1 tick above signal bar, sell 1 tick below. Market already moving your way. Best for most traders.

**Limit entries** (experienced traders only): Buy below the low of the prior bar, sell above the high of the prior bar. Entering against the move — requires strong context read. Common in channels: bulls buy below prior bar lows, bears sell above prior bar highs.

### How to Trade a Trend (Ch 18)

When in a trend, look for ANY reason to enter with-trend. Stop entry approaches:
- H2 pullback to MA (bull) / L2 pullback to MA (bear)
- Wedge flag pullback
- Breakout pullback after flag breakout
- H1 in strong spike (not after climax)
- Buy above prior swing high in very strong bull trend
- Sell below prior swing low in very strong bear trend

Limit entry approaches (experienced):
- Buy at bull trend line or prior swing low (potential double bottom bull flag)
- Sell at bear trend line or prior swing high (potential double top bear flag)
- Buy at/below weak low 1 or 2 signal in possible new bull trend
- Buy at/below low of prior bar in quiet bull flag at MA

**The simple existence of a trend is reason enough to enter at least a small position at market.**

## 8. Spike & Channel

```
Spike: ████ ████ ████    → strong, one-sided, momentum
Channel: ██ ██ ██        → two-sided, tails bigger, overlapping
TR: ════ ════            → trading range forms
```

- **Spike phase:** aggressive entries, no pullback needed. 1–5 strong trend bars.
- **Channel phase:** all bars = potential with-trend setups. Slower, two-sided.
- **After spike & channel** → usually TR → double bottom/top pattern.
- **Every trend bar is a climax or part of one.** Climax ends with pause/reversal bar, NOT necessarily opposite trend.

**Spike & channel is the fundamental market structure.** It happens to some extent every day. The start of the channel usually becomes the start of an incipient trading range. Bottom of channel phase gets tested within a day or two. Bears scale into shorts on pullbacks during channel; bulls return to buy at channel bottom → trading range broadens.

**All bull channels are functionally bear flags.** All bear channels are functionally bull flags. If the trend is strong, the breakout may go sideways and trend continues. Rarely, trend accelerates out of the channel — usually climactic and fails within 5 bars.

## 9. Breakouts, Trading Ranges, Tests, and Reversals (Ch 3)

**Breakout:** When the market breaks out into a trend, there is usually a trend bar (small or large) followed by many trending bars as the market spikes away from the trading range. The most important skill: reliably distinguish successful breakout from failed breakout (reversal).

**Test:** Market returns to an area of support/resistance — trend line, trend channel line, measured move target, prior swing high/low, entry bar low/high, signal bar high/low, yesterday's H/L/C/O. Bulls want to see a strong breakout on the test; bears want to see a reversal bar rejecting the area.

**Reversal:** Change from one type of behavior to opposite. Most commonly: trend → trading range (not trend → opposite trend). **Most reversals fail to lead to an opposite trend and instead become trading ranges.** Markets have inertia. Even when an opposite trend eventually unfolds, take at least partial profits at first reasonable targets.

**Reversals look different on different time frames.** A monthly bear reversal bar might be a weekly two-bar reversal, which might be a daily three-bar bull spike → 10-day TR → two-bar bear breakout. What matters is recognizing the pattern as a reversal on whatever chart you use.

## 10. Trend Lines (Ch 13)

**Bull trend line:** Drawn across lows of a bull trend. **Bear trend line:** Drawn across highs of a bear trend.

**Primary use:** Entries in trend direction on pullbacks. After trend line break, look for entries in opposite direction after test of extreme.

**Drawing:** Swing points or best fit (linear regression, visual). Can use candle bodies only (common in wedge patterns). Can create as parallel of trend channel line. Usually erase after verification — too many lines = distraction.

**Key rules:**
- Once trend established by trending H/L, most profitable trades are in trend line direction until it breaks.
- Every time market pulls back to trend line area (even if it undershoots/overshoots), look for reversal → enter with trend.
- After trend line break, trend extreme usually gets tested. Test can be followed by trend continuing, reversing, or entering TR.
- **Trend line break = first sign that market is no longer controlled by just one side.**
- Each successive trend line has flatter slope → trend losing momentum. Eventually opposite-direction trend lines become more important.
- If market repeatedly tests trend line many times in small number of bars: either breaks through (reversal) or accelerates away (trend continuation).
- Strength of trend line break indicates strength of countertrend traders.

**Most breakouts fail!** Strong sell-offs to bull trend line look like reversals — beginners short, experienced traders buy with limit orders at/below the trend line. Market needs to probe below to find information. Most of the time, more buyers appear and bull trend resumes.

## 11. Trend Channel Lines (Ch 14)

**In a bull channel:** trend line below lows, trend channel line above highs. In a bear channel: reversed. Same general slope.

**Primary use:** Fading a trend that has gone too far, too fast. Look for overshoot that reverses, especially second penetration.

**Drawing:** As parallel of trend line (drag to opposite side), or across swing points on opposite side, or best fit. For a parallel: anchor to a bar between the two trend line points that contains all bars between the lines.

**Wedge = convergent channel.** When trend line slope > trend channel line slope, urgency is building. Bulls buying on smaller pullbacks, bears exiting on smaller sell-offs. Creates wedge shape → reversal setup.

**Why overshoots cause reversals:** Last shorts give up and cover → spike up → euphoric bulls buy → process feeds on itself → not enough shorts left → trapped longs panic → market becomes one-sided dominated by sellers. Even if this narrative is imprecise, the pattern is entrenched in institutional trading psyche.

**Key:** Most trend channel line overshoots are also wedge reversals, even if the wedge isn't obvious. Smart traders won't trade countertrend until either: pullback from strong trend line break, OR reversal from trend channel line overshoot.

## 12. Channels (Ch 15)

**A channel is a sloping trading range.** Market is always in some kind of channel. Bull channel = bear flag. Bear channel = bull flag.

**Triangles are channels too:** Expanding triangle (two diverging trend channel lines), contracting triangle (two converging trend lines), ascending triangle (resistance above, bull trend line below), descending triangle (support below, bear trend line above). Wedge = converging variant.

**Vacuum effect:** Market races to top/bottom of channel because traders hold back, expecting the line to be reached. Creates sharp moves to channel extremes that attract naïve traders → institutional traders fade the move.

**Channel lifecycle:**
1. At start: trade with trend (buy below lows in bull channel).
2. As it develops more two-sided trading (overlapping bars, bear bodies, tails): experienced traders start fading.
3. Channels usually have at least 3 legs before ending.
4. Breakout from channel → measured move target = height of channel.
5. Most breakout attempts from channels fail.

**Strong channels (steep, tight, little overlap):** Risky to trade first breakout against trend. Better to wait for breakout pullback. If market rallies above MA and first pullback stays above MA → strong. If first pullback forms below MA → weaker.

## 13. Micro Channels (Ch 16)

**Definition:** A trend line drawn across 2–10 bars where most bars touch or are close to it, with a trend channel line on the opposite side. Extremely tight — no pullbacks or rare, small pullbacks. Not to be confused with a micro channel on a higher time frame.

**Strength indicators:** More bars, stronger trend bodies in direction, smaller tails → stronger micro channel → first pullback more likely to fail to reverse the trend.

**Trading micro channels:**
- **With-trend entry on failed breakout:** If bull micro channel in bull trend, first bar that pokes below the micro trend line = high 1 buy setup (failed breakout buy). Enter 1 tick above that bar's high.
- **Don't enter against:** Bear micro channel in bull trend at MA = bull flag. Don't short below it. Instead, buy the failure when the bear breakout fails.
- **Only use for with-trend setups.** Micro trend line shorts in bull trends near MA are losing trades.
- **10+ bar micro channel** = unsustainable climax → tradable reversal likely.

**Micro channel on 5-min chart = pullback on 1-min chart.** Don't need to check 1-min — read the 5-min and trade off that.

## 14. Horizontal Lines: Swing Points (Ch 17)

**On TR days:** Horizontal lines across prior swing highs/lows often serve as barriers → failed breakouts → reversals. Expect swing high breakouts to fail as HH reversals, swing low breakouts to fail as LL reversals. Fade of second HH or LL = even more likely to succeed (second signal).

**On trend days:** Use horizontal lines only to enter on pullbacks. E.g., after upside breakout of TR, pullback to breakout level + bull reversal = breakout pullback buy.

**Don't fade strong trends.** Only consider fading swing highs in bull trends or swing lows in bear trends if there was first a good trend line break AND a strong reversal bar. Even then, only scalp.

## 15. Pattern Evolution

> "Reliable patterns fail about 40% of the time and often evolve."

| Original | Evolves Into |
|----------|-------------|
| Failed L2 short | Wedge top |
| Failed final bear flag | Breakout pullback sell → larger TR |
| Spike & channel | TR → double bottom bull flag |
| Double top bear flag | Double bottom bull flag (opening hour) |
| Expanding triangle 5 legs | 7 legs |
| Micro TL breakout fails | Breakout pullback |

**Agent rule:** Setup failed? Check for trapped traders → fuel for opposite move. After many sideways bars → new pattern is independent.

## 16. Day Type Classification

| Day Type | Characteristics | Trading Approach |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Strong Trend / Trend from Open** | Direction from bar 1, small pullbacks | All with-trend signals, enter at market. Best reversal bars = traps. |
| **Trending TR** | Opening range 1/3–1/2 avg range, breakout after 1-2h | Both directions, swing with-trend bias |
| **Reversal Day** | Trends one way → reverses toward close | Second entry after reversal bar. Strong last-2h reversal = follow-through next day. |
| **Trend Resumption** | Strong trend → tight TR → resumption | Anticipate resumption, enter at first pullback. Leg 2 ≈ Leg 1. |
| **Stairs / Broad Channel** | 3+ trending swings, each pullback overlaps prior breakout | With-trend entries only, expect next-day continuation. Shrinking = reversal likely. |

### Reversal Day (Ch 24) — Detailed

- Most start as trending trading range days.
- If reversal starts in last couple of hours and is strong → follow-through next day and often several days.
- Some strongest trends begin mid-day or end of day as TR breakouts or climactic reversals.
- Recognize early: pullback that has strong countertrend spike → trend may be reversing (not just a bear flag).
- Restrict trading to new direction once recognized.

### Trend Resumption Day (Ch 25) — Detailed

- Strong trend first hour → TR for hours → trend resumes final 1-2 hours.
- Trading range is often very tight — don't give up on boring midday.
- Often a brief 1-2 bar breakout that fails between 11am-noon PST (trap), followed by real breakout in trend direction.
- Trend resumption can span multiple days: strong move → TR lasting 1-2 days → resume.
- Second leg often ≈ first leg in size.

### Stairs / Broad Channel (Ch 26) — Detailed

- At least 3 trending swings resembling mildly sloping TR/channel.
- Each breakout followed by pullback beyond breakout point (overlap between consecutive swings).
- Both bulls and bears active, can trade both directions, but swing with-trend.
- **Shrinking stairs:** Each breakout smaller than prior → waning momentum → deeper pullback or reversal likely.
- Pay attention to how many ticks breakouts run past recent swing → use that to fade subsequent breakouts.

## 17. Risk & Trade Management

| Element | Rule |
|---------|------|
| **Stop** | 1 tick beyond signal bar extreme |
| **Scaling** | Scalp portion + swing portion |
| **Swing target** | Measured move of prior leg |
| **Scalp target** | At breakeven or 1× risk |
| **Breakeven stop** | Move after 1× risk secured |
| **Trailing stop** | Below prior swing low (bull) / above prior swing high (bear) |
| **Late entry** | Same stop as original entry |
| **Max loss** | Defined by Trader's Equation, not fixed |

**Trader's Equation:** Only take a trade when prob × reward > (1 − prob) × risk. If probability is 60%, don't take profit until market has moved at least as far as your risk. Most traders should swing, not scalp — scalping requires ~70% win rate.

**Don't change plans mid-trade.** Swing traders who scalp out after losses miss the big wins they need. Scalpers who swing after watching a trade become a trend get stopped out on the next trade. Stick to your plan.

**In strong trends:** Trail stop below most recent swing low. Raise to breakeven after pullback tests entry then new high. Don't tighten to below a doji entry bar — 50/50 it gets hit.

## 18. 20-bar EMA

- Default moving average on ALL timeframes.
- Strong bull: price stays above, pullback to EMA = M2B entry.
- **MA gap bar:** bar not touching EMA → first one = buy setup for test of extreme.
- **20 gap bars:** 20+ consecutive bars not touching MA → strong trend → first touch = setup for test of extreme.
- M2B / M2S = H2 / L2 at EMA.
- If market has not pulled back to MA in 20+ bars: buy with limit order at MA, scale in lower.

## 19. Decision Vocabulary

| Decision | Meaning | Conditions |
|----------|---------|------------|
| **LONG (↑)** | Enter long | With-trend setup, signal bar, trend aligned |
| **SHORT (↓)** | Enter short | With-trend setup, signal bar, trend aligned |
| **WAIT** | Watching zone | Not at entry yet, note exact level |
| **FORGET** | Skip entirely | Low prob, unclear structure, no R:R |
| **WATCH** | Sideways but interesting | Monitor for catalyst or breakout |

**Terminology:**
- LONG = bullish bet (price UP). SHORT = bearish bet (price DOWN).
- Do NOT use "BUY"/"SELL" — always LONG/SHORT.
- Do NOT say "SHORT scenario" when meaning "sell existing long" — that is not SHORT.

### Mandatory Call Format

```
TICKER — LONG/SHORT

TP1: xxx  (R:R ~x:x)
TP2: xxx  (R:R ~x:x)

```

## 20. ETFs and Inverse Charts (Ch 9)

**SPY** is almost identical to Emini chart, sometimes has clearer price action due to smaller tick size.

**SDS** (UltraShort S&P 500, 2× inverse) — if a bull flag on Emini looks like a rounding bottom on SDS, don't buy the flag. Wait for more price action.

**Triple witching days:** SPY has larger gap opening than Emini due to price adjustment, but trades tick-for-tick after that. Don't be concerned by the gap.
